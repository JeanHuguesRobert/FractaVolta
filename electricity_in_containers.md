# Electricity in Containers

## A Note on Store-and-Forward Energy Logistics

**Jean Hugues Noël Robert, baron Mariani**
Institut Mariani — C.O.R.S.I.C.A.
Working Paper — April 2026

---

## Abstract

Electricity is conventionally conceived as a continuous flow transmitted through fixed conductors. This paper argues that this ontology is incomplete.

As electrification expands across mobility, heating, and digital infrastructures, an overlooked complementary possibility emerges: electricity may also be treated as a transportable stock, moved in modular containers across multiple scales.

From portable batteries and electric vehicles to transportable battery containers, such systems suggest a latent logistics layer for electricity, complementing rather than replacing wired grids.

This note proposes a conceptual framework for **containerized electricity** and introduces the notion of **store-and-forward energy logistics**, drawing cautious analogies with buffering and packetized communication architectures. It is argued that this perspective may illuminate neglected pathways for resilience, distributed flexibility, and reduced marginal pressure on conventional network reinforcement.

---

## 1. Introduction

Modern electrical systems are built upon an implicit assumption:

**electricity moves through wires.**

Even decentralized energy discourse—smart grids, distributed generation, storage—largely preserves this premise.

Yet electrification itself may be destabilizing this assumption.

Electric vehicles, modular batteries, and mobile storage suggest another possibility:

electricity may circulate not only as flow, but also as **transportable stock**.

This is not a claim that grids become obsolete.

It is a claim that electrical infrastructure may be richer than usually assumed.

---

## 2. Electricity as Flow and as Container

Historically, electricity has been treated primarily as instantaneous flux.

This paper proposes complementing this view by distinguishing two infrastructural modalities:

### Flow electricity

Transport through conductive networks.

### Containerized electricity

Transport through movable stored energy units.

Examples already exist:

* portable battery packs
* swappable batteries
* electric vehicles
* mobile battery trailers
* containerized storage systems

These are typically discussed as devices.

Here they are viewed as elements of a possible infrastructure layer.

---

## 3. Store-and-Forward Power

A useful conceptual lens may come from communication systems.

Packet-switched networks introduced **store-and-forward** logic:

information is buffered, moved, routed, and delivered through distributed nodes.

A limited analogy may apply to electricity.

Stored electrical energy may sometimes be:

* accumulated locally,
* physically transported,
* discharged elsewhere,
* routed through distributed assets.

Electric vehicles illustrate one instance, but only one.

In this sense, some electricity may be said to move not through continuous transmission, but through **store-and-forward power logistics.**

In an ironic sense, this suggests a practical form of wireless power transmission—not through resonant fields as imagined by Nikola Tesla, but through logistics.

---

## 4. Fractal Form Factors

One notable property of containerized electricity is scale recursion.

It appears naturally across multiple form factors:

| Scale        | Example                          |
| ------------ | -------------------------------- |
| personal     | portable battery modules         |
| household    | modular energy cartridges        |
| mobile       | electric vehicles                |
| neighborhood | transportable battery containers |
| regional     | mobile storage fleets            |

This suggests a potentially fractal architecture rather than isolated technical artifacts.

Such scaling deserves study.

---

## 5. Buffers, Caches, and Resilience

A second useful analogy comes from distributed computing.

Storage devices can be interpreted heuristically as:

* buffers absorbing intermittency,
* caches reducing backbone stress,
* distributed reserves improving graceful degradation.

The analogy should not be overextended.

But it highlights a neglected idea:

future energy resilience may depend not only on stronger centralized infrastructure, but on layered distributed buffering.

This resembles, in spirit, the robustness logic that informed early packet networks such as ARPANET.

---

## 6. Implications for Grid Reinforcement

Electrification is widely expected to require substantial reinforcement of transmission and distribution networks.

That may be true.

Yet it may be incomplete.

Where loads are flexible, storage distributed, and mobility electrified, some marginal reinforcement pressures may potentially be reduced through logistics-based flexibility rather than wires alone.

This is not an argument against grids.

It is an argument for broadening the design space.

Infrastructure may be:

[
Wires + Storage + Logistics
]

rather than wires alone.

---

## 7. Research Questions

This note is exploratory.

It raises more questions than it answers.

Among them:

* Under what conditions can mobile electricity logistics complement wired transport economically?
* How should containerized electricity be modeled?
* Can store-and-forward power improve resilience under stress?
* What regulatory assumptions prevent such architectures from emerging?
* Can this reduce marginal infrastructure investment requirements?

These questions appear underexplored.

---

## 8. Conclusion

The energy transition may involve more than decarbonized generation and reinforced grids.

It may involve a partially new logistics layer for electricity itself.

Electricity may not only flow.

It may be stored, moved, buffered, routed.

In part, it may circulate.

Recognizing this possibility may help illuminate a neglected dimension of future infrastructure.
